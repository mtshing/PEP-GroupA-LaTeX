\section{Kirchhoff's Law}

The two Kirchhoff's laws are the fundamentals to every circuit-solving problem. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)
    \item Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)
\end{itemize}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Kirchhof's Current Law}

%\quote{Sum of all current entering and exiting a node must equal to 0}

The same law has many other names depending on the text's background. 
For example,
\begin{itemize}
    \item Circuit junction rule
    \item Node rule
\end{itemize}

The physics of KCL is just conservation of charge - 
the amount of charge that goes into a circuit line junction 
must equal to the amount that comes out. 

\aleq{
    Q_\text{in} = Q_\text{out} 
    \quad\Rightarrow\quad 
    I_\text{in} = \dvv{Q_\text{in}}{t} = \dvv{Q_\text{out}}{t} = I_\text{out}
}

\insertFig{KCL}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Kirchhof's Voltage Law}

%\quote{Sum of voltage rises and drop around a closed path must equal to 0}

Similarly, the same law has many other names.
For example, 
\begin{itemize}
    \item Circuit loop rule
    \item Mesh law
\end{itemize}

The physics of KVL is just energy conservation - 
when a charge travels along any loop in a circuit,
the amount of electric energy it gains from voltage source 
must equal to the amount it dissipates through electronic components.

\aleq{
    E_\text{gain} = E_\text{loss}
    \quad\Rightarrow\quad
    V_\text{gain} = \frac{E_\text{gain}}{Q} = \frac{E_\text{loss}}{Q} = V_\text{loss} 
}

\section{Methods of Solving Circuit}

There are 3 different methods commonly found in textbooks:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Solving ODEs
    \item Phasor Diagram
    \item Impedance method
\end{itemize}

Directly solving with ODEs is the most fundamental approach,
but the calculation is the most annoying. 
So electronic engineers developed the latter two methods to simplify their jobs.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{System of ODEs}

In a circuit that only contains power sources, resistors, capacitors and inductors, 
we can express the voltage across each component as a time derivative of charge $Q$:
\aleq{
    \bcase{
        C = \frac{Q}{V} \quad&\Rightarrow\quad V_C = \frac{Q}{C}\\
        R = \frac{V}{I} \quad&\Rightarrow\quad V_R = RI = R\dvv{Q}{t}\\
        L = \frac{\epsilon}{\dvv{I}{t}} \quad&\Rightarrow\quad V_L = L\dvv{I}{t} = L\dvv[2]{Q}{t}
    }
}

Then the relations from KVL will become linear ODEs over $Q$,
and relations from KCL connect the charge conservation on each junction of the circuit.

\subsubsection{R-C Circuit}

\insertFig{RC circuit with switch}

When the circuit contains one resistor and one capacitor in series, 
the KVL writes as 
\aleq{
    \frac[Q]{C} + R\dvv{Q}{t} = V(t) 
} 

The solution depends on the function of the power source. 
The usual two cases we are interested in are
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \bf{Charging} - Initial charge on capacitor is $0$. 
    At time $t=0$, switch is closed and voltage sharply changes from $0$ to $V_0$.
    The solution for $t>0$ is 
    \aleq{
        \frac{Q}{C} + R\dvv{Q}{t} &= V_0\\
        Q(t) = CV_0 + Q_0e^{\frac{-t}{RC}}
    }

    \insertFig{power source step function at t=0, shade out the part t<0}

    \insertFig{charging curve}

    \item \bf{Discharging} - Initial charge on the capcitor is $CV_0$.
    At time $t=0$, switch is opened and voltage sharply changes from $V_0$ to $0$. 
    The solution for $t>0$ is 
    \aleq{
        \frac{Q}{C} + R\dvv{Q}{t} &= 0\\
    }
    
    \insertFig{power source step function at t=0, shade out the part t<0}
    \insertFig{discharging curve}

\end{enumerate}
